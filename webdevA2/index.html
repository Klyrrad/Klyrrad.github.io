History Of Bowling

5200 B.C.

The beginning of bowling

Bowling has been traced to articles found in tomb of an Egyptian child buried. Where primitive implements included nine piece of stone at which a stone "ball" was rolled, the ball having first to roll through an archway made of three pieces of marble.

Unknown

Ancient Polynesia

Polynesians play ula maika, a stone-rolling game involving pin-like targets 60 feet away-an early ancestor of modern tenpin Bowling.

200-400 A.D.

Ancient Germany

Bowling used a religious ritual. People rolled stones at clubs ("kegels") to cleanse themselves of sin. Martin Luther later codifies the game with nine pins, promoting it for recreation.

1300s-1500s

Europe

Bowling spreads across England, the Netherlands, and Scandinavia. Various forms arise - lawn bowling, skittles, and ninepins.

1600s

England & Colonies

Bowling is common in England. It's brought to the American colonies by European settlers. Ninepins became the dominant form in the U.S.

1841

Ninepin banned from some U.S. States

Ninepin bowling is outlawed in states like Connecticut due to its association with gambling. Tenpin bowling was then borned by adding one extra pin to circumvent the law.

1875

National Bowling Association (USA)

First national attempt to unify rules, formed by clubs in New York. Short-lived due to disputes over regulations.

1895

American Bowling Congress (ABC) Established

Standardizes rules, equipment, and lane dimensions. First organized governing body for men's bowling in the U.S.

1901

First ABC National Championship Tournament

Held in Chicago, the tournament becomes an annual tradition and the most prestigous in U.S. Bowling.

1916

Women's International Bowling Congress (WIBC) Formed

After early women's leagues and tournaments, WIBC is established to govern women's competitive bowling.

20th Century (1930s-1950s)

Technological Advances

Introduction of automatic pinsetters, synthetic lanes, and standardized scoring to revolutionize the game. That is where bowling became a mainstream recreational activity across the U.S.

1950s-1970s

Bowling Boom

Explodes in popularity with the rise of televised tournaments and the Professional Bowlers Association (PBA) was founded in 1958. Bowling alleys become widespread social centers.

2005-Current

Formation of United States Bowling Congress (USBC)

ABC, WIBC, and other groups merge into USBC, the national governing body for both amateur and competitive bowling in the U.S.

Types of Bowling

Ten Pin Bowling

Ten-pin bowling is a type of bowling in which a bowler rolls a bowling ball down a wood or synthetic lane toward ten pins positioned evenly in four rows in an equilateral triangle. The objective is to knock down all ten pins on the first roll of the ball a strike, or failing that, on the second roll a spare.

Bowling Ball Guide

Bowling Ball Cores

Symmetrical

A symmetrical core in a bowling ball refers to a core with a uniform shape, where the mass is distributed equally around the ball's center. This results in a predictable and consistent ball motion down the lane, making it ideal for bowlers seeking control and repeatability.

Bowling Ball CoverStock

Solid CoverStock

Solid coverstocks are characterized by their matte finish, devoid of any glossy sheen (unless polished). These coverstocks provide maximum traction on the lane surface, generating significant friction for an early and strong hook motion. The lack of polish creates a higher friction coefficient, allowing the ball to grip the lane surface more aggressively, especially in heavy oil conditions. Solid coverstocks excel in providing a smooth and predictable arc, making them suitable for bowlers who prefer a controlled motion with a strong mid-lane read. They are particularly effective on fresh oil patterns where a consistent hook is crucial for performance.

Bowling Ball Numbers

Radius Of Gyration

The RG (Radius of Gyration) number of a ball indicates how readily it will roll or hook. Lower RG numbers (e.g., 2.46-2.51) mean the ball will roll earlier and potentially hook sooner, while higher RG numbers (e.g., 2.57-2.80) indicate the ball will roll later and potentially hook further down the lane.

Key Elements of Oiling Patterns

Length

Refers to the distance of the oil applied on the lane, and it significantly impacts a bowler's strategy and ball selection. Shorter patterns (e.g., 35 feet or less) cause the ball to hook earlier, while longer patterns (e.g., 43 feet or more) allow the ball to slide farther before hooking. Adjusting speed, ball choice, and line are crucial for success on different lengths

Rule of 31

The "Rule of 31" in bowling is a guideline for estimating the breakpoint of the bowling ball on a given oil pattern. Suggests that you subtract 31 from the length of the oil pattern to determine the approximate board where the ball should exit the pattern.

For example, on a 45 foot pattern, the breakpoint would be 14 board (45 - 31 = 14). This rule is a starting point and doesn't account for lane surface or individual bowler characteristics.
